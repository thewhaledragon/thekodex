For years I told myself I was almost ready to write. I had drafts. I had outlines. I had folders full of notes labelled "someday." What I didn't have was a published post, because I kept waiting for the idea to be perfect before I committed words to the page.

The breakthrough came when I finally understood something obvious in hindsight: **the idea doesn't become good before you write it. It becomes good because you write it.**

## The perfectionism trap

Perfectionism disguises itself as high standards. But there's a difference between caring about quality and refusing to start. One produces excellent work eventually. The other produces nothing.

> The first draft is just you telling yourself the story.

When you accept that the first draft is supposed to be rough, you free yourself to actually begin. You're not writing the finished piece — you're writing your way toward understanding what the finished piece should be.

## What changed for me

I started setting a single constraint: write for 25 minutes without editing. Not without thinking — just without going back. Forward motion only. At the end of 25 minutes, I had something to work with. It was never as bad as I feared, and often contained one or two ideas I hadn't planned but genuinely liked.

From there, editing felt like a different job — calmer, more surgical. The internal critic could come in now. It had earned it. But it wasn't allowed in the room while I was drafting.

## The only rule

Start before you're ready. Ship before it's perfect. Let the work teach you what it wants to be. You'll never write anything worth reading by thinking about writing.
